Red Velvet Cake

Ingredients

Sponge

  • 340 g sunflower oil, plus extra for greasing
  • 240 g buttermilk
  • 2 large eggs
  • 1 tsp vanilla extract
  • 1 Tbsp white wine vinegar
  • ½ - 1 red food colouring tube, as desired (see tip)
  • 300 g caster sugar
  • 300 g plain flour
  • 20 g cocoa powder
  • 1 ½ tsp bicarbonate of soda
  • ¼ tsp fine sea salt

Icing

  • 180 g caster sugar
  • 250 g cream cheese
  • 40 g unsalted butter, soft
  • ½ tsp vanilla extract

Preparation

Sponge

  1. Preheat oven to 170°C. Grease and line bases of 2 loose-based cake tins (Ø 20 cm) with baking paper.
  2. Place oil, buttermilk, eggs, vanilla extract, vinegar and food colouring in mixing bowl then mix 30 sec/speed 3.
  3. Add sugar, flour, cocoa powder, bicarbonate of soda and salt then mix 20 sec/speed 5. Scrape down sides of mixing bowl with spatula then mix again 20 sec/speed 5.
  4. Divide evenly between prepared tins. Bake for 40-45 minutes (170°C) until a skewer inserted in centre comes out clean. Leave to cool in tins for 10 minutes before removing to a wire rack to cool completely. Meanwhile, clean mixing bowl and make icing.

Icing

  1. Place sugar in mixing bowl and grind 20 sec/speed 10.
  2. Add cream cheese, butter and vanilla extract then mix 30 sec/speed 4. Scrape down sides of mixing bowl with spatula then mix again 10 sec/speed 4. Transfer to a bowl and set aside. Clean mixing bowl.

Assembly and Decoration

  1. Once sponges are cool, cut a thin layer of sponge off the top of one cooled cake and place in pieces in mixing bowl. Grate 2-4 sec/speed 6 until fine crumbs form.
  2. Spread a layer of reserved icing over the exposed sponge then place second sponge on top. Ice top and sides of cake with remaining icing then scatter reserved sponge crumbs over sides and rim of cake.
  3. Transfer to a serving plate or cake stand then serve in slices.

Useful Items

skewer, wire rack, baking paper, loose-based cake tins (Ø 20 cm), oven

Hints & Tricks

  • An instantly recognisable tea-time treat, red velvet cake was first served at the Waldorf Astoria Hotel in New York. The characteristic sponge originally came from using a red cocoa powder, however, red food dye is more commonly used today. We suggest using food colouring gels or pastes as the resulting colour will be deeper and more intense than that achieved from the liquid variety. Finished with an irresistible cream cheese icing, this cake will be a hit with everyone!
  • Different food colourings have different strengths. Adjust quantity according to preference.
  • Adding vinegar and buttermilk helps the raising agent, in this instance bicarbonate of soda, work effectively.
