Chawanmushi (Japanese savoury custard)

Ingredients

  • 1050 g water
  • 1 tsp dashi powder (see Tips)
  • 4 eggs
  • 2 tsp soy sauce
  • 1 tbsp rice wine
  • ¼ tsp salt
  • 3 fresh shiitake mushrooms, stems removed and cups cut into slices (approx. 5 mm)
  • 8 peeled raw prawns (approx. 100 g)
  • 2 spring onions/shallots, cut into thin slices

Preparation

  1. Place 450 g of the water into mixing bowl and heat 9 min/100°C/speed 1.
  2. Add dashi powder and mix 5 sec/speed 4 or until dissolved. Transfer into a jug and set aside to cool completely (approx. 1 hour ).
  3. Place eggs into mixing bowl and mix 10 sec/speed 2.
  4. Add cooled dashi stock, soy sauce, rice wine and salt and mix 10 sec/speed 2, or until combined. Strain custard mixture through a fine mesh sieve into a jug and set aside. Rinse mixing bowl.
  5. Divide mushrooms and prawns equally between 4 ramekins (6-8 cm, ensuring ramekins fit comfortably in Varoma dish). Gently pour custard mixture into ramekins and cover each ramekin with aluminium foil.
  6. Place remaining 600 g water into mixing bowl. Place Varoma dish into position and place ramekins into Varoma dish. Secure Varoma lid and steam 12 min/Varoma/speed 1. Remove Varoma lid and carefully rotate custards to ensure even cooking. Secure Varoma lid and steam 6 min/Varoma/speed 1, or until custards are set and a toothpick inserted into the centre comes out clean. Carefully remove from Varoma and garnish with spring onions/shallots and serve warm.

Useful Items

jug, fine mesh sieve, aluminium foil, toothpick, ramekins (6 cm)

Hints & Tricks

  • Chawanmushi meaning "tea cup" or "steamed in a bowl", is an egg custard dish popular in Japan. It is usually eaten as a starter or part of a larger meal. And it is one of the few traditional Japanese dishes eaten with a spoon!
  • Dashi is a Japanese stock or broth made from kombu (dried kelp), Bonito flakes (dried and smoked skipjack tuna shaved into thin flakes), anchovies, sardines or a combination of all of the above. Dashi is a fundamental ingredient in many Japanese dishes and can be found at Asian grocers and some major supermarkets.
