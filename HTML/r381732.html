Baby's first Christmas

Ingredients

  • 1700 g pork loin, rolled and skin scored (see Tips)
  • extra virgin olive oil, for rubbing
  • sea salt, for sprinkling
  • ground black pepper, for sprinkling
  • 8 Granny Smith apples, cut into halves
  • 500 g water, plus extra if needed
  • 250 g fresh green beans, trimmed

Preparation

  1. Preheat oven to 220°C. Set aside a large roasting pan or baking dish.
  2. Rub skin of pork with olive oil and sprinkle with salt and pepper. Place pork into baking dish and roast for 20-30 minutes (220°C), until skin starts to bubble. Reduce oven temperature to 180°C and continue to roast pork for a further 30 minutes.
  3. Carefully remove roasting pan from oven and add apples to pan. Coat apples in pan juices. Return to oven to roast for a further 15-20 minutes (180°C), until apples are almost tender. Increase oven temperature to 220°C for 10-15 minutes, until crackling is golden and crisp, pork is just cooked through and apples are tender. Set aside to rest for 10 minutes. Proceed with recipe while pork cools.
  4. Place water into mixing bowl. Insert simmering basket, weigh beans into it and steam 10-12 min/100°C/speed 2 or until beans are cooked. Remove simmering basket with aid of spatula and set aside. Discard water and dry mixing bowl.
  5. For your baby's serve, place 80 g pork (omitting crackling), 200 g apple (skin and core removed) and 80 g green beans into mixing bowl and blend 1 min/speed 7 or until desired consistency is achieved. If needed, stir through a little water to get the consistency just right before serving to your baby. Slice remaining pork and serve with apples and beans.

Useful Items

large baking dish, pastry brush, oven

Hints & Tricks

  • Baby's serve: follow steps in recipe to prepare baby's puree. For an older baby keep the blended consistency more textured or cut some pork into strips and allow your baby to enjoy this meal as finger food.
  • Toddler's serve: serve as is, cut into appropriate sized portions.
  • I prefer to buy local, free-range, organic pork wherever possible, free from added hormones and growth promoters. This might mean paying more, but I would prefer to do that and eat pork less regularly, knowing that the pig has had a better life and my family's health is also better for it.
