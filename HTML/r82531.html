Chelsea Buns

Ingredients

Dough

  • unsalted butter, for greasing
  • 400 g strong white bread flour, plus extra for dusting
  • 220 g milk
  • 1 tsp ground mixed spice
  • 3 tsp fine sea salt
  • 2 tsp dried instant yeast
  • 2 Tbsp caster sugar
  • 1 medium egg

Filling

  • 50 g unsalted butter, soft
  • 50 g caster sugar
  • 75 g currants
  • ½ tsp ground mixed spice

Drizzle

  • 50 g icing sugar
  • water, to mix

Preparation

Dough

  1. Grease a baking tin (30 cm x 24 cm x 6 cm) then set aside. Place flour, milk, ground mixed spice, salt, yeast, sugar and egg in mixing bowl then mix 20 sec/37°C/speed 3. Knead 3 min/ then leave dough in mixing bowl until doubled in size (approx. 1-2 hours).
  2. After this time, knead 30 sec/ then transfer dough to a well-floured surface (dough will be sticky and very soft). Sprinkle with flour then roll into a rectangle (approx. 30 cm x 50 cm) using a flour-dusted rolling pin.

Filling

  1. Spread 50 g soft butter over dough with a soft spatula. Fold bottom third up to cover middle third and top third down over this. Give dough a quarter turn so that the fold is on the left and roll out again into a rectangle, then fold into three again.
  2. Roll into a rectangle (approx. 30 cm x 50 cm), adding flour to surface as required to stop dough sticking. Spread sugar, currants and mixed spice over dough. Roll up into a sausage shape then cut into 12 equal slices.
  3. Place slices cut side up, about ½ cm apart, in prepared tin. Cover with cling film and leave to rise in a warm place until doubled in size (approx. 1 hour). Towards the end of proving time, preheat oven to 200°C.
  4. Bake for 25-30 minutes (200°C). Allow buns to rest in tin while making drizzle.

Drizzle

  1. Place a small bowl on mixing bowl lid and weigh in icing sugar. Remove bowl and add water 1 tsp at a time to make a thin icing.
  2. Drizzle over buns while still warm then remove from tin and serve warm or at room temperature.

Useful Items

baking tin (30 cm x 24 cm x 6 cm), rolling pin, soft spatula, cling film, small bowl, pastry brush, oven, oven

Hints & Tricks

These sticky, fruity buns are synonymous with British baking and date back to the eighteenth century. Glazing the buns while still hot gives them a sweet, sticky finish.
