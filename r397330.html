Poached eggs with hollandaise

Ingredients

Hollandaise sauce

  • 100 g butter, cut into cubes (1-2 cm)
  • 3 egg yolks
  • 1 tbsp lemon juice
  • sea salt, to taste
  • ground black pepper, to taste

Poached eggs

  • 1900 g water
  • 4 whole eggs
  • 1 bunch asparagus, woody ends trimmed
  • 1 tbsp fish roe, to serve (ocean trout or salmon - see Tips)
  • 1 tbsp fresh chives (chopped) or micro greens, to serve

Preparation

Hollandaise sauce

  1. Place a bowl onto mixing bowl lid, weigh butter into it and set aside.
  2. Place yolks, lemon juice, salt and pepper into mixing bowl and emulsify 1 min/70°C/speed 3.
  3. Cook for a further 5 min 30 sec/70°C/speed 3, without measuring cup, slowly adding butter, 1 cube at a time, through hole in the mixing bowl lid. Transfer into a small thermal serving bowl or other small bowl and cover to keep warm. Clean and dry mixing bowl.

Poached eggs

  1. Place water into mixing bowl. Insert simmering basket, then heat 5 min 30 sec/50°C/speed 2 or until temperature reaches 50°C.
  2. Place eggs into simmering basket (the temperature will drop a little after eggs are added) and cook 13 min/70°C/speed 3 or until yolks have started to thicken (see Tips). Remove eggs and set aside (see Tips).
  3. Remove enough water from mixing bowl so approx. 1L remains. Re-insert simmering basket. Place asparagus into simmering basket and steam 5 min/100°C/speed 3 or until tender.
  4. To serve, crack each egg into a small bowl (simply crack on the midline as you would a raw egg) and top each with ½ -1 tablespoon of the Hollandaise sauce (see Tips), 1 teaspoon of the fish roe and 1 teaspoon of the chopped chives or micro greens. Serve with asparagus.

Hints & Tricks

  • Baby’s serve: for a younger baby, you can blend together some of the egg and asparagus until smooth, or wait until your baby is managing some finger foods (which may not happen until 8-9 months or older) before offering them this meal. Cut the asparagus into lengths that your baby can easily hold.
  • Toddler’s serve: serve as is, with asparagus cut into smaller lengths.
  • Fish roe is gaining recognition as an excellent source of omega-3 fats (DHA) in ‘phospholipid’ form, which research suggests might be the most bioavailable form. DHA is linked to improved brain function and a lower dementia risk so it’s a nutrient I like my whole family to have regularly. I keep a jar of roe in the fridge and use it in place of a fish oil supplement. You can find ocean trout roe at specialty food stores, selected fishmongers and online.
  • Using asparagus dippers, rather than toast, adds a boost of green to your morning. Asparagus is also a particularly good choice for gut health – it has high levels of inulin, a special kind of ‘prebiotic’ that encourages the growth of good bacteria in your gut.
  • The eggs will continue to firm from their residual heat when they are set aside, so if you crack them straight after removing them from the mixing bowl, they may not be cooked through. If using straight away, you may wish to cook them for an extra 30-60 seconds. Furthermore, egg cooking times can vary depending on the size of your eggs and whether they have been brought to room temperature before cooking. For food safety, cook the eggs at least until the whites are cooked through and the yolks have started to thicken.
  • You can store any leftover Hollandaise sauce in a sealable container in the refrigerator for up to 2 days; simply reheat before serving.
